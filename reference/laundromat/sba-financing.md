# SBA financing

How SBA 7(a) financing interacts with laundromat acquisition. Used by Job 5 (SBA financing feasibility) and referenced from Jobs 1, 3, 4 where relevant.

**Authoritative source on every rule below: the buyer's SBA lender + SBA SOP 50 10 V8 (effective 2025-06-01).** This file summarizes publicly-indexed rules to support screening; binding interpretation belongs to the lender. Gate 1 (binding-advice refusal in `rules.md`) applies.

---

## SBA 7(a) baseline eligibility

Per the SBA's public eligibility page, the business must:

- "Be an operating business"
- "Operate for profit"
- "Be located in the U.S."
- "Be small under SBA Size Requirements"
- "Not be a type of ineligible business"
- "Not be able to obtain the desired credit on reasonable terms from non-Federal, non-State, and non-local government sources"
- "Be creditworthy and demonstrate a reasonable ability to repay the loan"

**Maximum loan amount:** $5 million.

Laundromats are not on the SBA's ineligible-business list. The category is well-suited to 7(a) financing and lenders have substantial experience with it.

## §first-time-individual

The primary buyer sub-segment for this specialist.

### What lenders typically require

| Requirement | Typical band | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Down payment | 10% – 20% of project (purchase price + closing + working capital) | 10% is the SBA minimum; lenders often want more for first-time buyers |
| Personal credit score | 680+ typical floor; 700+ improves terms | |
| Liquid reserves at close | 3–6 months operating expenses post-down-payment | Lenders verify before commitment |
| Personal financial statement (PFS) | Comprehensive; all assets + liabilities | Updated within 90 days |
| Industry experience | Not required for laundromat (operationally simple); lenders prefer some adjacent business experience | |
| Personal guarantee | Required from all 20%+ owners | Joint-and-several with any partners |
| Citizenship / immigration status | US Citizen or Lawful Permanent Resident (LPR) for owners providing personal guarantees | Per publicly-summarized SOP 50 10 V8; verify with lender |

### Common decline reasons for first-time individual buyers

1. **Personal credit issues** — recent delinquencies, bankruptcies within 7 years, judgments, tax liens
2. **Insufficient liquidity** — down payment + reserves cannot be assembled from documented sources
3. **Listing-side issues** — see §listing-side-decline-risks below
4. **Industry change-of-control concerns** — lender's prior bad experience with the category (uncommon for laundromat)
5. **Buyer's plan is unconvincing** — buyer cannot articulate operating plan, transition plan, or year-1 financials

## §E-2-section

E-2 visa buyers (non-immigrant treaty-investor visa holders) face a structural challenge with SBA 7(a) financing.

### The constraint

SBA SOP 50 10 V8 requires that owners providing personal guarantees on a 7(a) loan be US Citizens or Lawful Permanent Residents (LPRs). E-2 holders are neither. **This is the controlling rule the buyer's SBA lender will apply.** Verify with the lender — the buyer's immigration attorney is the secondary check.

### Paths E-2 buyers actually use

| Path | Mechanism | Trade-offs |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **All-cash** | E-2 investor funds the purchase directly without a 7(a) loan | No financing-contingency risk; faster close; higher capital tied up; reduced reserves for year-1 surprises |
| **Cash + seller note** | Cash for portion of purchase + seller carries a note | Seller note is often subordinated; depends on seller's appetite; typical seller notes carry 5–7% interest and 5–7 year amortization |
| **Partnership with US Citizen / LPR guarantor** | E-2 buyer holds a non-guarantor minority equity position; US-citizen/LPR partner provides personal guarantee | Complicates control rights; requires careful operating agreement; partner's personal guarantee creates partner-side risk and reward |
| **Commercial bank loan (non-SBA)** | Conventional commercial loan | Higher down payment (typically 25–35%); shorter amortization (5–7 years typical); higher rate; rarely available for first-time individual buyers under $1M |

The specialist surfaces these paths when Job 5 fires for an E-2 buyer. The specialist does **not** advise on visa-eligibility implications of any structure — that is the buyer's immigration attorney's call.

### E-2 buyer screening differences

- **Lease-assignability risk weighted higher** — visa renewal cycles intersect with lease term; an E-2 buyer who can't renew their visa at the same time their lease is up has a forced-exit risk
- **Equipment-CapEx tolerance lower** — less cushion for unplanned year-1 CapEx given thinner reserves typical for cash-financed deals
- **Operator-attention assumption higher** — E-2 buyers typically must demonstrate active management for visa maintenance; remote-operator / passive-investor structures may not satisfy USCIS

## §second-loan-dynamics

For the regional clusterer sub-segment (2–5 stores already).

### What changes from first-loan dynamics

- **Cross-collateralization** — second SBA loan may cross-collateralize with prior SBA loans, depending on lender policy
- **Aggregate SBA exposure** — single borrower's aggregate SBA-loan exposure is capped at $5M (the 7(a) limit). Across 2–5 stores, the limit can become binding
- **Personal guarantee on subsequent loans** — joint-and-several across all SBA loans of the borrower
- **Lender relationship** — having a prior 7(a) with a specific lender often expedites the second-loan process with that lender, but reduces lender-choice flexibility
- **Demonstrated operator track record** — second-time SBA borrower has the prior store's P&L as evidence; lenders weight this heavily

### Clusterer screening differences

- **Portfolio-fit primary screen** before financial screen — does this listing complement existing stores, or cannibalize?
- **Existing-portfolio CapEx schedule overlaid** — if 2 stores in portfolio also have equipment-age clusters, year-2 CapEx becomes concentrated
- **Management-bandwidth** — adding a store often requires either a hire or a route-reorganization; cost of either should be in year-1 P&L

## §revenue-verification

The interaction between revenue verification and SBA underwriting.

### What SBA underwriting will accept

- **Card-system transactional data** + **bank deposit history** reconciled across ≥24 months → high-confidence; lender accepts as-stated
- **Hybrid card + coin** with reconciled coin-pull frequency tied to deposit history → medium-confidence; lender may discount stated SDE by 5–10%
- **All-coin with extrapolated revenue** → low-confidence; lender will likely discount stated SDE substantially (15–30%) or decline
- **Cash-not-deposited add-backs in SDE** → SBA auto-flag category; lender will reject these add-backs from the SDE used for underwriting

### What this means for the buyer

If the listing has cash-not-deposited add-backs in SDE, the SDE used for SBA underwriting is materially lower than the stated SDE. The deal that "pencils" on stated SDE may not pencil on underwriting-SDE.

The specialist flags this in §red-flags-checklist as the auto-PASS revenue-verification flag and recommends pre-LOI CPA reconciliation.

## §lease-term-rule

### The rule (publicly indexed)

SBA generally requires the lease (including options the buyer can exercise unilaterally) to match or exceed the loan amortization term. Laundromat-secured 7(a) loans are typically 10-year amortization.

### What this means in practice

- Lease term ≥10 years total (base + buyer-controlled options) → meets SBA requirement
- Lease term 6–9 years total → SBA may require shorter loan amortization (8 years instead of 10), which raises monthly debt service and stress-tests the cashflow
- Lease term <6 years total → SBA will likely decline or restructure significantly

### Diligence path

Lease in full → transactional attorney's review surfaces actual term + option mechanics. Pre-LOI lender conversation confirms the lender's specific lease-term policy.

## §pre-LOI-lender-conversation

### The discipline

Before submitting any SBA-financed LOI, the buyer should have a 30–60 minute conversation with their selected SBA lender walking the listing's structural facts.

### What to walk

- Headline economics (ask, revenue, SDE)
- Lease term + structure
- Equipment-age range (rough; not yet inspector-verified)
- Payment system
- Anything that looks unusual (E-2 sub-segment, anchor-attached, prior dry-cleaner site, customer concentration, recent renovation, distressed seller)

### What to ask

- *"Would underwriting flag any of these?"*
- *"Is the lease term workable for a 10-year amortization?"*
- *"What's the expected timeline from accepted LOI to commitment?"*
- *"What pre-application materials should I have ready?"*

### Why this is load-bearing

Lender-flagged structural issues surface AFTER earnest money is at risk only if the buyer skips this conversation. Pre-LOI conversation is free; surfacing post-LOI is expensive.

## §listing-side-decline-risks

Listing-side reasons SBA-financed deals get declined or restructured. These are the screening flags most-likely-to-kill the financing path.

1. Lease term < amortization (most common)
2. Revenue not verifiable (cash-not-deposited add-backs; coin-only with no deposit history)
3. Customer concentration >40% of revenue from a single source
4. Active litigation or regulatory violations
5. Prior environmental contamination (Phase 1 reveals concerns)
6. Owner-is-the-business with no transition plan
7. Equipment fleet at end-of-life with no CapEx reserve plan
8. Buyer's personal financial profile (covered separately in §first-time-individual)

## §rate-and-fees

Approximate ranges for SBA 7(a) loans (US, 2024–2026). These shift with prime rate; verify with the lender.

| Item | Typical band |
| --- | --- |
| Interest rate | Prime + 1.5% to Prime + 4.75% (for loans <$5M) |
| Amortization | 10 years for goodwill / equipment / general working capital; 25 years if real estate is part of collateral |
| SBA guarantee fee | 0% to 3.5% of guaranteed portion depending on loan amount and policy year |
| Bank fees | Typically 1–2% of loan amount |
| Closing costs | ~2–3% of purchase price including legal, environmental, title |
| Personal guarantee | Required from all 20%+ owners |
